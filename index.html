The lots that keep being harvested across Central Harlem

More than places to be, rescued empty lands in this area are orchards maintained by volunteer work, mostly known as community gardens. Except for one or another vacant lot, most spaces between housing complexes are being used this way, which draws attention to the role that an integrated recreational area can play in a residential zone.

By Gabriela A. Carrasquillo Piñeiro

July 31, 2022

In a crowded city such as New York City, Central Harlem is no different than other residential areas in Manhattan: tall and narrow buildings with their characteristic brick color and outside design, side by side street after street. And unlike most recreational areas, “the birth of Pocket Parks in Harlem wasn’t included in the urban planning programs of [the city], but it was a product for the city people wanted to satisfy the necessity to have a space for everybody where people can meet during the day” (Francesco Armato, 2017).

It takes a walk across 120th and 135th streets in this area to notice that most missing buildings in this residential zone are not empty lots but mostly beautiful green areas with planting terraces enclosed by a black gate, populated with at least one tomato plant and a picnic table.

Once known as “Vest-Pocket Parks”, these small areas in between structures were never meant as spaces of passive recreation, but a space of cultural convergence designed by the people to respond to the needs of the community. A close look at the usage of each one of these spaces, it can be noticed how the majority of them are Community Gardens, and only a few are passive parks.

El rescate de lotes para su uso como espacios públicos se remonta a 1964, y estuvo respaldado por todo un movimiento que inició esa década tras la construcción del primer Vest-Pocket Park en toda la Ciudad de New York, en la calle 128 de Central Harlem.

Tras este evento, hubo un boom, según reportó el New York Times, en que los oficiales gubernamentales locales impulsaron la construcción de más pocket parks en verano del año 1965.

Meses después, residentes de Central Harlem cuestionaron la intervención del gobierno en una iniciativa que había surgido de las comunidades al señalar el peligro de que el Estado quisiera tomar estos proyectos en sus manos.

A pesar de que la mayoría son parte del movimiento de NYC Parks Greenthumb del New York City Department of Parks and Recreation, todas son gestionadas por voluntarios residentes del área y aún sobreviven iniciativas completamente independientes.
